Introduction

FLYING PRINCIPLE

  • how to make a quadrotor hover a certain position
  • what motion command you have give to the motors to make it move in a particular direction
  • a quadrotor consists of four rotor blades. each of these rotors actually

produces a certain air flow and in turn an acceleration force onto the body of the quadrotor.

  • if you fire up all the four motors at a certain speed,

then you can take off.

  • if the thrust of all the four motors combined together exactly compensates for earth gravity, then the quadrotor will vertically stay in place.
  • make sure that the torques of all the four motors sum exactly to zero. If not, all the four rotors would rotate in the same direction, then that would induce a contour-torque that would start to rotate the quadrotor around it's yaw axis.

torques 0

  • You always have two motors, the opposing motors mounted on the same arm that are rotating in the same direction, and two rotors, the other two motors rotate in the opposite direction.
  • trust compensates for earth gravity
  • just increase equally the speed of all four motors
  • reduce the speed equally of all four motors

Brief History on Quadrotor Research

fixed-wing airplanes

  • need runway when take-off and landing-on to generate enough speed
  • can not stop in the air

quadrotors

  • 1924
  • vertical take-off
  • hard to stabilize the flight
  • 1936: the first helicopter
    • one main rotor
    • relatively easy to navigate manually
    • second tail rotor that then compensates for the torque
    • disadvantage: main swash plate in the middle is too large and dangerous.
  • change: new MEMS sensor that appeared in the end of 90s and the beginning of 2000s
  • enable hobbyists to develop micro aerial vehicles that could do the attitude stabilization on board using such sensors. And then that lead to the development of the first remote controlled quadrotors.

2D GEOMETRY

robot odometry

  • often we want to estimate the robot motion from it sensors or from information that we have.
  • First of all, of course we know typically what motion commands we gave to the robot, of course we never know exactly whether these controls have been executed properly, but we can make certain predictions about the resulting robot motion that we would expect.
  • control-based
  • odometry based
  • velocity-based
  • wheel robots: wheel spins
  • velocity sensor
  • down looking camera
  • integration of odometry is also called dead reckoning
  • a mathematical procedure to determine the present location of the vehicle its odometry readings, whatever those are.
  • This can be usually achieved by using the estimated or measured velocities and integrating that over the elapsed time.

motion models

  • how the controls or the IMU readings or velocity readings from your sensor can be translated into the robot motion.
